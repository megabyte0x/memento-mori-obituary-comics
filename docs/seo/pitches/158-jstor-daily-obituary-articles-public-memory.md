Hi JSTOR Daily editors,

I would like to pitch a public-scholarship piece about obituary articles as a bridge between family memory, public record, and archival evidence.

The piece would be grounded in JSTOR-accessible scholarship and primary-source collections on obituary culture, mourning images, memorial notices, newspaper death notices, family history, and public memory. It could work as a short pedagogical essay or reading-list style piece for instructors, librarians, and students who want to study how lives become public records.

One contemporary example for context is Memento Mori Obituary Comics, which treats obituary articles as source-backed visual biography rather than as generic memorial copy:

The article would not be promotional. The core question would be: what can obituary articles teach students about evidence, privacy, public history, and the way ordinary lives enter the archive?
