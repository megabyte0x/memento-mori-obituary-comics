## Subject

PITCH: When obituary articles leave the local-news desk

## Email

Hi Nieman Lab editors,

I would like to pitch a reported story for Nieman Lab about a small but revealing future-of-news question: what happens when obituary articles move outside the local-news desk and are rebuilt by families, AI tools, memorial platforms, grief publications, and visual storytellers?

The story would not be an op-ed or a product profile. It would use obituary articles as a concrete case study in how a public-interest journalism form changes when local-news capacity declines and the work shifts to platforms, families, and independent creators.

The reporting plan could include:
- a local-news obituary editor or former obituary desk reporter;
- a journalism educator who teaches obituary or feature writing;
- a memorial-platform or death-tech operator;
- a family historian or librarian who sees obituary records used after publication;
- and Memento Mori Obituary Comics as one source-backed visual obituary example:
https://www.finalnotes.page/obituary-articles/

The wider question is how readers judge trust, sourcing, privacy, and completeness when the obituary becomes a digital artifact rather than a newspaper notice.
