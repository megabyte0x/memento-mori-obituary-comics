## Pitch Draft

Dear Medical Humanities Blog editor,

I am writing to ask whether you would consider a short guest essay on obituary articles, illness narrative, and the ethics of narrative closure.

Working title: "After the chart goes quiet: obituary articles and the ethics of narrative closure"

Proposed format: guest blog post, 700-800 words.

The essay would examine what happens when clinical facts, public death notices, family memory, and digital publication all meet in an obituary article. Obituaries often translate private medical timelines into public narrative. That process can honor a person, but it can also simplify illness, expose private details, or force a clean arc onto a life that was not cleanly resolved.

The piece would use visual obituary storytelling as a practical case for a broader medical humanities question: how can families, writers, clinicians, and editors preserve a life story while respecting privacy, ambiguity, and consent?
