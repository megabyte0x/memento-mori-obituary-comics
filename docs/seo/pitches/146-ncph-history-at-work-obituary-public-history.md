## Subject

Pitch for History@Work: When obituary articles become public history

## Email

Hi History@Work editors,

I would like to pitch a short History@Work post about obituary articles as a form of public history practice.

Working title:
"When obituary articles become public history"

The piece would examine what happens when families, local institutions, and independent storytellers try to preserve lives after local obituary desks and community newspapers have shrunk. It would treat obituary articles as more than announcements: they can become public records, community memory work, and contested decisions about what belongs in public and what should stay private.

The post would connect this practice to public history questions:

- who gets to interpret an ordinary life in public;
- how families and small institutions handle consent, privacy, and evidence;
- how visual obituary stories and source-backed narrative obituaries can supplement, but not replace, formal records;
- what public historians can learn from family memory work.

I would reference Memento Mori Obituary Comics only as one contemporary example of source-backed obituary storytelling:
