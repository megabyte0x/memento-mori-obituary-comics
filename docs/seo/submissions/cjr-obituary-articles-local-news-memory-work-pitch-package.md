## Subject

Pitch: When obituaries leave the local-news desk

## Email

Hi CJR editors,

I would like to pitch a reported media essay for CJR on what happens when obituary articles move beyond the local-news desk.

Working headline: When obituaries leave the local-news desk, what standards follow them?

The crux:
As local newsrooms shrink and families publish more memorial content directly through funeral-home, newspaper, newsletter, podcast, and memorial-site channels, obituary articles are drifting away from the editors who traditionally enforced verification, context, and public/private judgment. The piece would ask what journalistic standards should follow the form: who gets interviewed, what details require confirmation, which private facts should stay out, and how a life can be made legible without becoming a generic tribute.

Why now:
CJR recently covered The Obit Project and the renewed energy around obituary storytelling. That raises a wider media-practice question: if obituaries remain one of journalism's oldest forms, what happens when the form is rebuilt by students, families, independent writers, audio producers, and visual storytellers outside the conventional newspaper obituary desk?

Reporting plan:
- Speak with obituary reporters or local-news editors about how they distinguish a reported obituary article from a paid death notice or family tribute.
- Speak with journalism educators who teach obituary writing as a reporting exercise.
- Compare newspaper obituary articles, funeral-home notices, audio obituary projects, and source-backed visual obituary formats.
- Use Memento Mori Obituary Comics as one working example of a visual obituary format, then report outward to people who can address standards, ethics, and newsroom practice.
- End with practical lessons for editors and journalism instructors: verification, source notes, sensitivity around cause of death, family review, public record, and narrative focus.
