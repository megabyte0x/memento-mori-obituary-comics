## Email

Hi Poynter editors,

I would like to pitch a reported craft essay for Poynter on what visual obituary articles can teach reporters about summing up a life without flattening it into a notice.

Working headline: What visual obituary articles can teach reporters

The piece would start from a tension Poynter has already covered well: ordinary lives often disappear from public memory as local newsrooms shrink, even though feature obituaries can build loyalty, preserve community memory, and teach reporters accuracy under emotional pressure. Visual obituary articles add another lens to that problem. They force the writer to choose one verified scene, object, place, or turning point, then decide what evidence belongs in public and what should stay private.

The story would use Memento Mori Obituary Comics as one working example, but the focus would be journalism craft: how obituary articles balance speed, source trails, family memory, public record, visual detail, and sensitivity after a death.

Reference page:
https://www.finalnotes.page/obituary-articles/

Reporting plan:
- Interview or draw from reporters/editors who have written feature obituaries or local death stories.
- Compare conventional death notices, feature obituaries, and visual obituary structures.
- Examine how source notes, public records, family interviews, and visual details can help prevent generic tribute writing.
- Include practical takeaways for journalism educators, student reporters, and local-news editors.

Why now:
Local newsrooms continue to cut staff while families increasingly publish memorial content directly on funeral-home, newspaper, and memorial platforms. That makes obituary standards more important, not less. A craft essay could ask what still makes an obituary article journalism: verification, context, source sensitivity, public/private judgment, and a clear reason readers should care.

I can tailor this as a reported essay, teaching piece, or shorter craft column.
