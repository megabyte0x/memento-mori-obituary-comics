The crash broke the plan. The mirror helped build the work that remains.

Citable Summary

  • Frida Kahlo (1907–1954) was a Mexican painter whose self-portraits made pain, disability, identity, and death central evidence in modern art.
  • At age 18, Kahlo was badly injured in a bus-trolley crash in Mexico City; her long recovery helped turn painting from an interest into a life-defining practice.
  • This obituary comic frames the crash, the recovery bed, and the mirror as the conditions under which Kahlo began making the images that outlived her.

Story Notes

Frida Kahlo did not originally set out to build a public mythology from pain. She was a young pre-medical student before the 1925 bus-trolley crash left her with spinal, pelvic, shoulder, and foot injuries that shaped the rest of her life.

The comic uses the mirror as a practical and symbolic object: while confined during recovery, Kahlo turned her own body and face into available subjects. That constraint became one of the sources of her visual language.

The sources for this page combine a general reference biography, a museum artist profile, an art-history biography, and an editorial account of how Kahlo used clothing, posture, and self-presentation around disability.
